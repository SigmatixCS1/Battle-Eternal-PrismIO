# Scene 1 — Courtyard: The Kael Incident

The courtyard was loud with lunch-hour politics.

Riven Takar stood elevated on the stone ledge, speaking as though the academy had been waiting for him.

"It’s remarkable," he announced, "how disorganized leadership becomes without direction."

Several first-years nodded reflexively.

Across the courtyard, Kaelen Thorne sat alone, finishing his meal with mechanical precision.

Riven noticed.

And mistook stillness for submission.

He stepped down from the ledge and approached.

"You’re Thorne," Riven said, loud enough for nearby students to hear. "Security track. Impressive résumé. I imagine you’ll want to align with someone who understands institutional power."

Kael looked up once.

Not offended.
Not impressed.

Assessing.

"No," Kael replied.

The word was not hostile.
It was final.

Riven laughed lightly. "You misunderstand. I’m offering structure. You provide enforcement. Efficient."

Kael stood.

The shift in air pressure was subtle but immediate.

"I don’t enforce for strangers," Kael said.

Riven’s smile thinned. "Careful. Refusing my guidance could limit your future placement."

Kael stepped closer.

He did not touch him.

He didn’t need to.

The ground seemed heavier beneath Riven’s feet — gravity tilting just enough to make balance uncertain.

Students felt it but couldn’t name it.

Kael’s voice lowered.

"You’re not my future."

Riven attempted to reassert posture.

Kael moved.

It happened too fast for the courtyard to follow.

Riven was on the ground — not injured, not struck brutally — simply disarmed and immobilized in one precise, humiliating motion.

Kael released him immediately.

"Don’t escalate what you can’t control," Kael said.

Then he walked away.

Laughter broke out — not cruel, but unstoppable.

Riven stood slowly.

For the first time in his life, his name had not been enough.

# Scene 2 — Strategy Wing: The Crystalis Rejection

Riven approached Crystalis privately.

He believed privacy equaled advantage.

"You and I," he began smoothly, "should be collaborating. Your intellect deserves institutional protection."

Crystalis didn’t look up from her tablet.

"Protection from what?" she asked calmly.

"From irrelevance," he replied.

That made her look up.

Not interested.

Curious.

"You think you are structure," she said. "You are noise with a last name."

Riven’s composure cracked a fraction.

"Careful. My family has influence beyond this campus."

Crystalis smiled faintly.

"Then why are you here trying to impress students?"

Alexander, who had absolutely been listening from a nearby corridor, burst into visible laughter.

"Oh no," he said between breaths. "He thought this was a recruitment pitch."

Crystalis’ composure broke into one of her rare laugh fits.

Not polite.

Uncontrolled.

Riven flushed.

"You’ll regret this," he said quietly.

Crystalis’ expression returned to neutral instantly.

"No," she replied. "You will."

He left.

But something had shifted.

Elara, watching from across the hall, felt it first.

The humiliation didn’t dissolve.

It calcified.

# Scene 3 — Assembly Hall: Alexander Spectacle

Riven confronted Alexander publicly.

"You treat serious matters like theater," Riven said. "It makes you predictable."

Alexander tilted his head. "And you treat theater like a monarchy. It makes you funny."

Students watched.

Riven stepped closer.

"Do you know who my father is?"

Alexander beamed. "Desperately hoping you’ll tell me."

Crystalis snorted.

The room tipped toward laughter again.

Riven felt it — the same tilt as the courtyard. The same imbalance.

He saw Alexander as the cause.

He did not see the pattern.

He did not see that the elite students were not orbiting him.

They were orbiting something else entirely.

He left that day with three realizations:

1. His name had no gravitational pull here.
2. The Elite did not answer to legacy.
3. Alexander Harukaza felt like interference.

The resentment did not flare.

It condensed.

And it began to look for leverage.
