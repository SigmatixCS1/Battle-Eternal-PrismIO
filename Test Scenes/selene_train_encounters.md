# Version A — Aligned Canon Pass

## Selene on the Train — Iron Class Car

Selene Anastas did not ride the train. She endured it.

The carriage was crowded with bodies, but the noise was not physical. Dreams pressed against her skin — unfinished conversations, falling sensations, remembered arguments looping in soft psychic echoes.

Most students thought Astral Chorus trained singers.
They did not understand it trained filters.

She kept her shields raised — thin, practiced partitions that turned overwhelming tides into manageable currents.

Then the sound stopped.

Not quieted.
Not dimmed.
Removed.

The psychic pressure in the car vanished as if the world itself had been edited.

Selene’s head lifted.

Three seats away: a boy in a dark hoodie, glasses reflecting the window light, a leather journal resting on his knee.

To sight — ordinary.
To dream-sense — absence.

Her breath caught.

Every mind carried residue. Even the disciplined ones leaked fragments. Images, metaphors, emotional color — something.

He had none.

He was not silent.

He was missing.

She reached carefully — a trained brush, not intrusion.

Contact.

The world tore open.

Not thoughts.
Not memory.

A sky collapsing in a geometry she could not parse. A pressure beyond emotion. A sound like structure itself failing. Entire meaning systems dissolving into static comprehension — not pain, not fear — termination.

Selene recoiled violently, severing the link.

The train returned in pieces: metal, light, breath, sound.

The boy turned a page.

He had never noticed her.

Selene pulled her coat tighter.

She had felt dreams before.
Nightmares before.
Death before.

This was none of them.

This was what remained after dreaming stopped.

# Version B — Symbolic Sharpened Pass

## Selene on the Train — The Ice and the Ocean

Selene Anastas walked across the surface of the world the way others walked across ice — carefully, knowing what lived beneath.

The Iron Class carriage floated on a sea of sleep. Every passenger trailed currents behind their eyes. Fear tasted metallic. Regret hummed warm. Hope flickered like distant lanterns under water.

She knew how to breathe here.

Then the ocean vanished.

Not calmed.
Not frozen.

Drained.

She looked up.

A boy sat in the corner, turning pages in a book that did not belong to this reality.

No dreams clung to him.
No metaphors leaked from him.
No subconscious tides moved around him.

The water of the world bent around a hole where a person should be.

Curiosity betrayed her training.
She reached — lightly, barely touching the surface of him.

She fell.

Not into darkness.
Into aftermath.

Stars extinguishing like files deleted mid‑sentence. Voices removed from history. A horizon folding into an error state existence could not recover from. Not terror — cancellation.

She ripped herself back into her body with a gasp.

The train snapped back into form: fluorescent light, rattling metal, murmuring students.

The boy adjusted his glasses and continued reading.

Selene stared at her hands, shaking.

She was used to nightmares.
Nightmares were human.

This was archival.

This was the memory of a universe that no longer had witnesses.
